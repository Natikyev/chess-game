Chapter 2
Rules & Basics
Every rule shown on a board. Chess has very few rules — but each one is important. These visuals show exactly what each rule looks like in practice.
Rule
Check & Checkmate
When your king is under attack, it is in check. You must escape immediately. Checkmate occurs when there is no legal escape — the game ends.
White queen delivers check. Black must respond.
Check — your king is attacked. Respond by moving the king, blocking the attack, or capturing the attacker. All three options must be considered.
Checkmate — the king is in check with no escape. The game ends immediately. You don't need to physically capture it.
Illegal moves — you can never make a move that leaves your own king in check under any circumstances.
⚠️ You cannot castle out of check, through a checked square, or into check.
Rule
Stalemate
Stalemate occurs when the player to move has no legal moves but is not in check. The game ends as a draw — even if one side is massively ahead.
It's Black's turn — but every square the black king could reach is attacked by White. The king is not currently in check.
Result: draw — stalemate immediately ends the game as a draw regardless of material advantage.
Avoid it when winning — the most common blunder in endgames. Always verify your opponent still has a legal move.
💡 When you're losing, try to engineer a stalemate to escape with a draw.
Black king has no legal moves — stalemate, draw.
Special Move
Castling
The only move where two pieces move at once. The king moves two squares toward a rook, and the rook jumps to the other side. Tucks the king to safety while activating the rook.
After kingside castling: king on g1, rook on f1.
Kingside (O-O) — king e1→g1, rook h1→f1. The short castle. Faster to execute.
Queenside (O-O-O) — king e1→c1, rook a1→d1. The long castle. Rook reaches the centre quickly.
Requirements — neither piece has moved, no pieces between them, king is not in check, king doesn't pass through or land on an attacked square.
⚠️ The rook CAN pass through an attacked square — only the king's path and destination matter.
Special Move
En Passant
A special pawn capture available only immediately after an opponent's pawn advances two squares from its starting rank, landing beside your pawn.
The situation — Black's pawn just moved d7→d5 (two squares). White's pawn is on e5, right beside it.
The capture — White's pawn moves to d6 as if Black's pawn only moved one square. The black pawn on d5 is removed.
Only immediately — this option expires after White's very next move. If White plays something else first, the right is gone permanently.
💡 En passant prevents pawns from "sneaking past" using the two-square move.
White can capture en passant on d6, removing d5 pawn.
Special Move
Pawn Promotion
When a pawn reaches the opposite end of the board, it must immediately be replaced by a queen, rook, bishop, or knight of the same colour. The pawn cannot stay a pawn.
White pawn on e7 is about to promote to queen.
Almost always promote to queen — it's the most powerful piece. Underpromoting is almost always wrong.
Underpromotion to rook — occasionally correct to avoid stalemate when a queen would trap the enemy king with no legal moves.
Underpromotion to knight — the knight can deliver checkmates that a queen geometrically cannot, due to its unique jump.
💡 You can have multiple queens — if your queen is still on the board, you get a second one.
Rule
Draw Conditions
Not every game ends in victory. Knowing when a draw is forced — and how to claim one — can save you in losing positions.
Stalemate — no legal moves, not in check. Automatic draw.
Threefold repetition — same position appears three times with the same player to move. Either player may claim a draw.
50-move rule — 50 consecutive moves with no pawn move and no capture. Either player may claim a draw.
Insufficient material — neither side can possibly deliver checkmate (K vs K, K+B vs K, K+N vs K).
Mutual agreement — either player may offer a draw at any time; if accepted, the game ends immediately.
K+B vs K — insufficient material, automatic draw.

Classical Opening
The Italian Game
One of the oldest openings in chess. White immediately controls the centre with e4, develops the knight to f3 and aims the bishop at f7 — the most vulnerable square in Black's starting position. Leads to rich middlegame positions with both tactical and strategic play at every level.
💡 After Bc4, keep developing before attacking. Castle kingside early. Never move the same piece twice in the opening without a concrete reason.

Sharp Defence
The Sicilian Defence
The most popular and combative reply to 1.e4. Black immediately fights for the centre with a flank pawn, refusing to give White a symmetric position. The imbalance gives both sides genuine winning chances — making it the weapon of choice for aggressive players who want to play for a win with Black.
💡 Black attacks the queenside while White attacks the kingside. Both sides play aggressively. This leads to some of the sharpest and most uncompromising chess ever played.

Solid System · d4
The London System
A reliable system for White that works against almost any Black setup. White builds a sturdy structure with pawns on d4 and e3, bishop on f4, and knight on f3. The position stays closed and safe — ideal for beginners who want a consistent setup they can learn once and play for life.
💡 The London's biggest advantage: memorise the first 8 moves and play them in almost any game. Use the saved energy to focus on your opponent's plan instead.

Strategic Defence
The French Defence
A solid and principled reply to 1.e4. Black immediately prepares to challenge White's centre with d5. The French leads to strategic battles with clear plans for both sides. Black's position can feel slightly cramped early, but it is rock-solid and full of powerful counterattacking potential.
💡 In the French, patience is everything. Your structure is solid. Develop, castle, and then launch a powerful queenside or central counterattack. Don't panic if White has more space early.
The Four Opening Principles
PRINCIPLE 01
Control the Centre
Pieces in the centre control the most squares. Play e4 or d4 on move one whenever possible.
PRINCIPLE 02
Develop Your Pieces
Get knights and bishops toward active squares. Don't move the same piece twice without a concrete reason.
PRINCIPLE 03
Castle Early
Move your king to safety behind pawns. Aim to castle within the first 10 moves of every game.
PRINCIPLE 04
Don't Rush the Queen
The queen can be chased by cheaper pieces, wasting your tempo. Develop knights and bishops first.
Chapter 4
Tactics & Patterns
Tactics decide games. Each pattern is shown on a board — study the position until the idea is clear. Recognising these in your own games is a skill built through repetition.
Fork
The Fork
One piece attacks two or more enemy pieces simultaneously.
Knight on d5 forks king & queen
The opponent can only save one piece — you win the other. Knights are the best forking pieces due to their unique L-shaped movement. Even pawns can fork two pieces.

Always scan for knight jumps attacking a king and a valuable piece simultaneously.
⚡ After placing your knight, immediately check if it attacks two valuable pieces.
Pin
The Pin
A piece that cannot move without exposing a more valuable piece behind it.
Bishop on b2 pins knight against king
Absolute pin — the shielded piece is the king. Moving the pinned piece is literally illegal. Relative pin — the shielded piece is valuable; moving the pinned piece is strategically unwise.

Pile more attackers onto a pinned piece — it can't run away.
⚡ Bishops and rooks are powerful pinning pieces. Look for pieces in line with the enemy king.
Skewer
The Skewer
Like a pin in reverse — the more valuable piece is in front.
Rook attacks king; queen behind is lost
The valuable piece (often the king) must move to escape the attack — revealing a less valuable piece behind it, which is then captured.

Example: Rook attacks king. King moves. Undefended queen behind it is captured.
⚡ Look for skewers when your rooks or bishops have open lines to the enemy king or queen.
Discovery
Discovered Attack
Moving a piece reveals an attack from the piece behind it.
Knight moves → rook checks king, knight hits queen
When you move a piece, you uncover an attack from the piece behind it. The moved piece can ALSO attack something — creating two simultaneous threats the opponent can only address one of.

Discovered attacks are most lethal when the revealed attack is a check.
⚡ Two threats at once. The opponent can only answer one.
Double Check
Double Check
Two pieces check the king simultaneously — only the king can move.
Knight & bishop both give check to king
A type of discovered attack where BOTH the moved piece AND the revealed piece give check simultaneously. The defender cannot block or capture — only the king can move. Often immediately fatal.

Double checks frequently lead to checkmate on the very next move.
⚡ Always calculate discovered checks — they often win the game on the spot.
Zwischenzug
Zwischenzug
An "in-between" move that changes the calculation entirely.
Check before recapturing
Instead of the expected recapture, you play a forcing move first — usually a check or new threat. This disrupts your opponent's plan before continuing your intended sequence.

Mastering the zwischenzug separates good tacticians from great ones.
⚡ Before recapturing, always ask: "Is there something even better first?"
Chapter 5
Endgame Essentials
The endgame begins when most pieces are gone. These principles will win games beginners draw and save games beginners lose. Each rule is shown on a board.
Centralised king dominates
Activate Your King
In the endgame, the king transforms from a liability into a powerful fighting piece. Once queens are traded off, march it toward the centre immediately. A centralised king controls up to 8 squares and participates in both attack and defence. A passive back-rank king loses endgames that should be drawn.
⭐ The king is a fighting piece in the endgame. Centralise it immediately.
Passed pawn marches forward
Passed Pawns
A passed pawn — one with no enemy pawns blocking it or on adjacent files — is a massive endgame asset. It threatens to promote to a queen. Use your king to escort it, advance it aggressively, and force the opponent to commit resources to stop it — leaving the rest of the board undefended.
⭐ "A passed pawn must be pushed!" — Nimzowitsch
Rook behind the passed pawn
Rooks Belong Behind Passed Pawns
Place your rook behind a passed pawn — yours OR your opponent's. Behind your own: the rook gains more scope as the pawn advances. Behind the opponent's: the rook restrains and can eventually capture it. Placing a rook in front of a passed pawn wastes its activity entirely.
⭐ Rook behind the passed pawn — yours or theirs.
White king holds the opposition
The Opposition
Two kings are in opposition when they face each other with one square between them and it is the other player's turn. The player not to move holds the opposition and can force the opposing king to step aside — essential in king-and-pawn endgames where escorting a pawn to promotion requires pushing the enemy king out of the way.
⭐ Gain the opposition to push the enemy king back and promote your pawn.
Any Black move worsens their position
Zugzwang
Zugzwang (German: "compulsion to move") is a position where any move you make worsens your situation — you would rather pass, but the rules require you to move. Most common in endgames. Put your opponent in zugzwang to force their king away from a critical square, letting your pawn advance to promotion.
⭐ Force zugzwang to convert close endgames into wins.
Trade pieces; keep your pawns
Simplify When Winning
When ahead in material, trade pieces (especially queens) to reach a clean endgame where your advantage is decisive. Avoid trading pawns — you need them to promote. When behind, do the opposite: avoid trades, keep the position complex, and create chaos that might cause your opponent to go wrong.
⭐ Winning: trade pieces, not pawns. Losing: avoid trades entirely.
